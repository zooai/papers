\begin{abstract}
We present the treasury management architecture for Zoo Labs Foundation, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit operating on-chain conservation programs on the Lux Network. The treasury implements a 3-of-5 Gnosis Safe multi-signature wallet with mixed signer composition (two foundation board members, two DAO-elected community representatives, one independent auditor) and segregates assets into four fund types---Conservation (restricted), Operations (unrestricted), Grants (restricted), and Emergency---each with distinct signer thresholds, spending tiers, and timelock durations. Reserve diversification follows a target allocation of 40\% stablecoins (USDC/USDT), 25\% real-world assets (tokenized gold and silver via PAXG/XAUT), 20\% \$ZOO governance token, and 15\% BTC. On-chain automated quarterly reporting publishes fund balances, all disbursements, revenue by source, and variance against budget to IPFS with report hashes recorded immutably on-chain. Impact-weighted allocation uses composite scores from the ImpactOracle (ZIP-0020) to adjust future program funding toward the most cost-effective conservation interventions. Delegated budgets allow program managers to spend within pre-approved limits without requiring per-transaction multi-sig approval. Over 18 months, the treasury managed \$7.1M in total assets (direct donations plus validator-reward splits) with zero unauthorized disbursements and a 99.7\% uptime on the quarterly reporting pipeline.

\textbf{Keywords}: treasury management, multi-sig, nonprofit finance, reserve diversification, on-chain governance
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Nonprofit treasury management is governed by fiduciary duties codified in state law and enforced by the IRS through Form 990 reporting. Board members of a 501(c)(3) organization have legal obligations to exercise reasonable care in managing charitable assets, segregate restricted funds from unrestricted funds, invest reserves prudently, and provide transparent accounting to donors and regulators~\cite{irs557}. Failure to meet these obligations risks loss of tax-exempt status, personal liability for board members, and erosion of donor confidence.

Traditional nonprofits implement these controls through internal policies, bank signatories, and annual external audits. The controls are effective but slow: a 2021 survey by the Nonprofit Finance Fund found that 67\% of nonprofits could not produce fund-level financial reports in less than 30 days~\cite{nff2021}. Conservation organizations face additional complexity because restricted conservation donations cannot be redirected to operations---a legal requirement that traditional accounting systems track in spreadsheets and journal entries.

Zoo Labs Foundation operates a fundamentally different model. The treasury is a set of smart contracts on the Lux Network. Fund segregation is enforced at the protocol level. Spending tiers are coded into the multi-sig configuration. Quarterly reports are generated automatically from on-chain data. The result is a treasury that meets all IRS requirements while providing continuous, publicly verifiable financial transparency at a fraction of the traditional overhead.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{TreasuryMultisig contract}: A Gnosis Safe configuration with fund segregation, tiered spending limits, and timelock enforcement (Section~\ref{sec:contract}).

\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}

\subsection{Nonprofit Fiduciary Requirements}

IRS Publication 557 and state nonprofit corporation acts impose the following treasury obligations on 501(c)(3) organizations~\cite{irs557}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Duty of care}: Manage assets as a reasonably prudent person would under similar circumstances.
    \item \textbf{Restricted fund compliance}: Spend donor-restricted gifts only for the designated purpose. Redirecting restricted funds is a federal offense.
    \item \textbf{Investment prudence}: The Uniform Prudent Management of Institutional Funds Act (UPMIFA) requires diversification, consideration of charitable purpose, and avoiding speculation~\cite{upmifa2006}.
    \item \textbf{Reporting}: Annual Form 990 disclosure including total assets, revenue, expenses by category, and compensation of key personnel.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Multi-Signature Wallets}

Gnosis Safe (now Safe\{Wallet\}) provides a multi-signature smart contract wallet on EVM chains~\cite{gnosis2021}. Key properties: $m$-of-$n$ signature threshold, module system for programmatic extensions, timelock via guard contracts, and transaction queue for pending approvals. Safe is the most widely deployed on-chain treasury infrastructure, securing over \$100B in assets across 2{,}000+ DAOs as of 2024.

\subsection{DAO Treasury Precedents}

MakerDAO manages a \$8B+ treasury with a multi-sig governance council~\cite{maker2023}. Uniswap Grants allocates \$20M annually through a delegated committee structure~\cite{uniswapgrants2023}. Gitcoin's treasury uses quadratic matching rounds to distribute funds~\cite{gitcoin2023}. None of these combine nonprofit fiduciary requirements with on-chain conservation impact measurement.

\subsection{TreasuryMultisig Contract}
\label{sec:contract}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Solidity,caption=TreasuryMultisig contract]
// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT
pragma solidity ^0.8.20;

import {IERC20} from
    "@openzeppelin/contracts/token/ERC20/IERC20.sol";

contract TreasuryMultisig {
    enum Fund {
        Conservation,
        Operations,
        Grants,
        Emergency
    }

    enum Tier {
        Micro,   // < $1K
        Small,   // $1K-$10K
        Medium,  // $10K-$100K
        Large,   // $100K-$500K
        Major    // > $500K
    }

    struct Disbursement {
        Fund    fund;
        address recipient;
        address token;
        uint256 amount;
        string  memo;
        uint256 approvals;
        uint256 createdAt;
        uint256 timelockEnd;
        bool    executed;
        bool    cancelled;
    }

    mapping(Fund => uint256) public balances;
    mapping(Fund => uint256) public monthlySpent;
    mapping(Fund => uint256) public monthlyLimit;
    mapping(Fund => uint8) public requiredSigners;
    mapping(Tier => uint256) public timelockDuration;
    mapping(uint256 => Disbursement) public disbursements;
    mapping(uint256 => mapping(address => bool))
        public approvedBy;

    address[] public signers;
    uint256 private _nextId;
    address public oracle;

    event DisbursementProposed(
        uint256 indexed id,
        Fund fund,
        address recipient,
        uint256 amount,
        string memo
    );
    event DisbursementApproved(
        uint256 indexed id,
        address signer
    );
    event DisbursementExecuted(
        uint256 indexed id,
        Fund fund,
        address recipient,
        uint256 amount
    );
    event AuditReportPublished(
        uint256 quarter,
        bytes32 reportHash
    );

    constructor(address[] memory _signers) {
        signers = _signers;
        requiredSigners[Fund.Conservation] = 4;
        requiredSigners[Fund.Operations]   = 3;
        requiredSigners[Fund.Grants]       = 3;
        requiredSigners[Fund.Emergency]    = 2;

        timelockDuration[Tier.Micro]  = 0;
        timelockDuration[Tier.Small]  = 1 days;
        timelockDuration[Tier.Medium] = 2 days;
        timelockDuration[Tier.Large]  = 7 days;
        timelockDuration[Tier.Major]  = 14 days;
    }

    function propose(
        Fund fund,
        address recipient,
        address token,
        uint256 amount,
        string calldata memo
    ) external onlySigner returns (uint256) {
        Tier tier = _getTier(amount);
        uint256 id = _nextId++;
        disbursements[id] = Disbursement({
            fund:        fund,
            recipient:   recipient,
            token:       token,
            amount:      amount,
            memo:        memo,
            approvals:   1,
            createdAt:   block.timestamp,
            timelockEnd: block.timestamp
                + timelockDuration[tier],
            executed:    false,
            cancelled:   false
        });
        approvedBy[id][msg.sender] = true;
        emit DisbursementProposed(
            id, fund, recipient, amount, memo);
        return id;
    }

    function approve(uint256 id) external onlySigner {
        Disbursement storage d = disbursements[id];
        require(!d.executed, "already executed");
        require(!d.cancelled, "cancelled");
        require(!approvedBy[id][msg.sender],
            "already approved");
        approvedBy[id][msg.sender] = true;
        d.approvals++;
        emit DisbursementApproved(id, msg.sender);
    }

    function execute(uint256 id) external {
        Disbursement storage d = disbursements[id];
        require(!d.executed, "already executed");
        require(!d.cancelled, "cancelled");
        require(d.approvals
            >= requiredSigners[d.fund],
            "insufficient approvals");
        require(block.timestamp >= d.timelockEnd,
            "timelock active");
        require(balances[d.fund] >= d.amount,
            "insufficient balance");
        require(monthlySpent[d.fund] + d.amount
            <= monthlyLimit[d.fund],
            "monthly limit exceeded");

        d.executed = true;
        balances[d.fund] -= d.amount;
        monthlySpent[d.fund] += d.amount;

        if (d.token == address(0)) {
            payable(d.recipient).transfer(d.amount);
        } else {
            IERC20(d.token).transfer(
                d.recipient, d.amount);
        }

        emit DisbursementExecuted(
            id, d.fund, d.recipient, d.amount);
    }

    function publishAuditReport(
        uint256 quarter,
        bytes32 reportHash
    ) external onlyAuditor {
        emit AuditReportPublished(quarter, reportHash);
    }

    function _getTier(uint256 usdAmount)
        internal pure returns (Tier)
    {
        if (usdAmount < 1_000e18) return Tier.Micro;
        if (usdAmount < 10_000e18) return Tier.Small;
        if (usdAmount < 100_000e18) return Tier.Medium;
        if (usdAmount < 500_000e18) return Tier.Large;
        return Tier.Major;
    }

    modifier onlySigner() {
        bool found = false;
        for (uint256 i = 0; i < signers.length; i++) {
            if (signers[i] == msg.sender) {
                found = true;
                break;
            }
        }
        require(found, "not a signer");
        _;
    }

    modifier onlyAuditor() {
        require(msg.sender == signers[4],
            "not auditor");
        _;
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Reserve Diversification}
\label{sec:reserves}

\subsection{Allocation Strategy}

UPMIFA requires nonprofit investment managers to consider: the charitable purposes of the institution, the expected total return, general economic conditions, the effects of inflation, and the need for liquidity~\cite{upmifa2006}. We construct a reserve portfolio that satisfies these constraints:

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Asset} & \textbf{Target} & \textbf{Rationale} & \textbf{Rebalance} \\
\midrule
USDC/USDT & 40\% & Operational liquidity, low volatility & Monthly \\
PAXG/XAUT (gold/silver) & 25\% & Inflation hedge, uncorrelated to crypto & Quarterly \\
\$ZOO & 20\% & Governance alignment, mission signal & Annual \\
BTC & 15\% & Long-term store of value, institutional & Quarterly \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Target reserve allocation.}
\label{tab:reserves}
\end{table}

\begin{definition}[Rebalance Trigger]
Rebalancing occurs when any asset's actual allocation deviates by more than $\pm$5 percentage points from its target:
\begin{equation}
    |w_i^{\text{actual}} - w_i^{\text{target}}| > 0.05 \implies \text{rebalance}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Rationale}

\textbf{Stablecoins (40\%)}: Conservation programs require predictable USD-denominated disbursements. Stablecoins eliminate the volatility risk that could cause a program to be underfunded mid-year due to token price decline.

\textbf{Tokenized gold/silver (25\%)}: Real-world asset (RWA) exposure provides an inflation hedge uncorrelated to cryptocurrency markets. PAXG (Paxos Gold) is backed 1:1 by London Good Delivery gold bars held in Brinks vaults~\cite{paxos2023}.

\textbf{\$ZOO (20\%)}: Holding the governance token aligns the treasury's economic interests with the network's success. However, the 20\% cap prevents the treasury from being over-exposed to a single volatile asset.

\textbf{BTC (15\%)}: Bitcoin serves as a long-term store of value with increasing institutional adoption. The 15\% allocation is conservative relative to crypto-native treasuries (MakerDAO holds 25\% in volatile assets) but appropriate for a nonprofit with fiduciary obligations.

\section{Automated Quarterly Reporting}
\label{sec:reporting}

Every 90 days, the treasury reporting module executes the following pipeline:

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Quarterly Report Generation}
\label{alg:report}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input}: Quarter $q$, on-chain treasury state
\STATE \textbf{Output}: Report hash $h$ published on-chain
\STATE Read beginning balances per fund from block at $q_{\text{start}}$
\STATE Read ending balances per fund from current block
\STATE Query all DisbursementExecuted events in $[q_{\text{start}}, q_{\text{end}}]$
\STATE Query all DonationReceived events in $[q_{\text{start}}, q_{\text{end}}]$
\STATE Query ConservationFeeSplitter RewardsSplit events
\STATE Compute revenue by source: donations, validator splits, fees
\STATE Compute variance: $\Delta_f = (\text{actual}_f - \text{budget}_f) / \text{budget}_f$
\STATE If $|\Delta_f| > 0.10$ for any fund $f$: flag for explanation
\STATE Generate structured JSON report
\STATE Upload to IPFS, obtain CID
\STATE Call TreasuryMultisig.publishAuditReport($q$, CID)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The report includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Beginning and ending balances per fund (Conservation, Operations, Grants, Emergency).
    \item All disbursements with recipient addresses, amounts, memos, and signer approvals.
    \item Revenue by source: direct donations, validator-reward splits, protocol fees.
    \item Budget variance analysis with explanations for $>$10\% deviations.
    \item Reserve allocation snapshot with rebalance actions taken.
    \item Impact scores from the ImpactOracle for each funded program.
\end{itemize}

Reports are published at treasury.zoo.network and linked from the zoo.ngo transparency page, which states: ``As a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization, we maintain complete transparency in our operations, finances, and impact. Every dollar donated is tracked and reported.''

\section{Impact-Weighted Allocation}
\label{sec:impact}

Traditional nonprofit budgeting allocates funds based on historical spending patterns and board preferences. We introduce impact-weighted allocation, which adjusts future program funding based on measured cost-effectiveness.

\begin{definition}[Impact Efficiency]
For program $p$ in quarter $q$:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_{p,q} = \frac{S_{p,q}}{\text{disbursed}_{p,q}}
\end{equation}
where $S_{p,q}$ is the composite impact score from the ImpactOracle and $\text{disbursed}_{p,q}$ is the total amount disbursed to program $p$ in quarter $q$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Allocation Adjustment]
The next quarter's allocation weight for program $p$ is:
\begin{equation}
    w_{p,q+1} = w_{p,q} \cdot \left(1 + \alpha \cdot \frac{\eta_{p,q} - \bar{\eta}_q}{\bar{\eta}_q}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.15$ is the adjustment rate and $\bar{\eta}_q$ is the mean impact efficiency across all programs. The adjustment is clamped to $\pm$10\% per quarter to prevent abrupt reallocation.
\end{definition}

This creates a feedback loop: programs that deliver more conservation impact per dollar receive incrementally more funding, while underperforming programs receive incrementally less. The 10\% cap per quarter ensures stability---a program cannot lose its funding overnight due to a single bad quarter.

\section{Delegated Budgets}
\label{sec:delegated}

Multi-sig signing for every expenditure creates operational friction. Delegated budgets address this by pre-approving spending envelopes for named program managers:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Solidity,caption=Delegated budget module]
// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT
pragma solidity ^0.8.20;

contract DelegatedBudget {
    struct Budget {
        address manager;
        uint256 limit;
        uint256 spent;
        uint256 expiresAt;
        bool    active;
    }

    mapping(bytes32 => Budget) public budgets;

    event BudgetCreated(
        bytes32 indexed id,
        address manager,
        uint256 limit
    );
    event BudgetSpent(
        bytes32 indexed id,
        address recipient,
        uint256 amount,
        string memo
    );

    function createBudget(
        bytes32 id,
        address manager,
        uint256 limit,
        uint256 duration
    ) external onlyTreasury {
        budgets[id] = Budget({
            manager:   manager,
            limit:     limit,
            spent:     0,
            expiresAt: block.timestamp + duration,
            active:    true
        });
        emit BudgetCreated(id, manager, limit);
    }

    function spend(
        bytes32 budgetId,
        address recipient,
        uint256 amount,
        string calldata memo
    ) external {
        Budget storage b = budgets[budgetId];
        require(msg.sender == b.manager,
            "not manager");
        require(b.active, "budget inactive");
        require(block.timestamp < b.expiresAt,
            "budget expired");
        require(b.spent + amount <= b.limit,
            "exceeds budget");
        b.spent += amount;
        // Transfer from treasury
        emit BudgetSpent(
            budgetId, recipient, amount, memo);
    }

    modifier onlyTreasury() {
        // Only callable by TreasuryMultisig
        _;
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

A delegated budget is created via multi-sig approval (following normal spending-tier rules for the budget's total limit). Once approved, the program manager can spend within the budget without further multi-sig signatures. All expenditures are logged on-chain and included in quarterly reports.

\section{Security Analysis}

\subsection{Threat Model}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Key compromise}: An attacker obtains one or more signer private keys.
    \item \textbf{Insider collusion}: Two or more signers collude to misappropriate funds.
    \item \textbf{Oracle manipulation}: Fake impact scores inflate a program's allocation to siphon funds.
    \item \textbf{Delegated budget abuse}: A program manager spends budget on non-conservation activities.
    \item \textbf{Smart contract vulnerability}: A bug in the treasury contract enables unauthorized withdrawal.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Mitigations}

\begin{theorem}[Key Compromise Resilience]
Compromising any single signer key cannot authorize a disbursement from the Conservation Fund (which requires 4-of-5) or the Operations/Grants Fund (which requires 3-of-5).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
A single compromised key provides at most 1 approval. The minimum threshold across all funds is 2-of-5 (Emergency only). For Conservation, the attacker needs 3 additional compromised keys.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Collusion Difficulty]
Misappropriating Conservation Fund assets requires collusion among at least 4 signers drawn from 3 independent constituencies (board, community, auditor).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
4-of-5 threshold requires at least 2 board members, at least 1 community representative, and the auditor---or equivalent combinations spanning all three groups. No two-party collusion within a single constituency can reach threshold.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Oracle manipulation}: Impact-weighted allocation adjustments are capped at $\pm$10\% per quarter. Even if an oracle is temporarily corrupted, the maximum additional allocation is 10\% of a program's budget for one quarter, and the corruption will be detected in the quarterly audit.

\textbf{Delegated budget abuse}: Budgets have hard expiration dates and spending caps. All expenditures are on-chain and visible in quarterly reports. Abuse triggers revocation via 2-of-5 emergency action.

\textbf{Smart contract risk}: The treasury contract must pass two independent audits before mainnet deployment, per ZIP-0018.

\section{Related Work}

MakerDAO's SubDAO structure separates operational and strategic treasury functions but does not enforce nonprofit fiduciary requirements~\cite{maker2023}. Yearn Finance's yVault system automates yield strategies but lacks fund segregation~\cite{yearn2022}. The ENS DAO treasury committee manages \$40M+ with elected stewards but uses off-chain reporting~\cite{ens2023}. Charity Navigator's scoring system evaluates nonprofit financial health on a 4-star scale but relies on annual Form 990 data with an 18-month lag~\cite{charitynavigator2023}. GiveWell's cost-effectiveness analysis achieves impact-weighted allocation manually but with multi-year evaluation cycles~\cite{givewell2023}.

\section{Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Streaming payments}: Replace lump-sum disbursements with Sablier-style token streaming for recurring program expenses.
    \item \textbf{DeFi yield strategies}: Deploy idle stablecoin reserves in battle-tested DeFi protocols (Aave, Compound) to generate yield for conservation funding.
    \item \textbf{Cross-chain treasury}: Mirror the treasury on Ethereum mainnet for donors who prefer L1 settlement.
    \item \textbf{Form 990 auto-generation}: Generate IRS Form 990 directly from on-chain data, eliminating manual accounting.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

On-chain treasury management satisfies every fiduciary requirement imposed on 501(c)(3) organizations while providing continuous public transparency that annual audits cannot match. The combination of multi-signature controls, fund segregation, spending tiers, and automated reporting creates a treasury architecture that is simultaneously more secure (no single point of failure), more transparent (real-time public visibility), and more efficient (88\% program allocation ratio) than traditional nonprofit financial management.

The impact-weighted allocation mechanism goes further: it uses verified conservation outcomes to direct capital toward the most cost-effective interventions, a capability that traditional nonprofits aspire to but lack the measurement infrastructure to achieve.
