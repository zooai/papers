\section{Implementation Status}
\label{sec:impl-status}

We state plainly what is built and tested versus what is designed, so the paper's claims
can be checked against code. The Freivalds verifier core and the two determinism
prerequisites it depends on are \emph{implemented and unit-tested} in the inference
engine; the commitment layer, the forward-pass integration, and the two architecture
extensions of Section~\ref{sec:families} are \emph{designed in this paper, not yet built}.

\subsection{Built and tested: the Freivalds core}

The matmul verifier of Section~\ref{sec:freivalds} is implemented as a backend-agnostic,
dependency-free module (\texttt{hanzo-engine/src/poi.rs}). It realizes the construction
exactly as specified: arithmetic over the Mersenne field $\mathbb{F}_p$ with
$p = 2^{61}-1$, run on the engine's \emph{exact integer accumulator} (the
i8$\times$i8$\to$i32 quantized path), with the Freivalds identity $A(Br) = Cr$ evaluated
by left/right matrix--vector products and deterministic challenge derivation. Being
dependency-free of the forward pass, its soundness is unit-testable in isolation. The
suite is \textbf{6/6 passing}, and each test pins a claim from
Section~\ref{sec:freivalds}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
\item \texttt{test\_honest\_matmul\_passes} --- an honest $C = AB$ passes every challenge
vector (zero false rejection; the exact-integer guarantee).
\item \texttt{test\_tampered\_output\_is\_caught} --- flipping a single output entry
($50 \to 51$) is caught (Theorem~\ref{thm:freivalds-one} soundness).
\item \texttt{test\_larger\_random\_matmul\_catches\_sparse\_cheat} --- in a
$16\times24 \cdot 24\times16$ product, a single buried off-by-one in one deep entry is
caught; this is the \emph{sparse-cheat} case that motivates challenging every layer
(Theorem~\ref{thm:soundness}, part~2).
\item \texttt{test\_negative\_entries\_field\_reduction} --- signed int8
weights/activations reduce correctly into $[0,p)$ (Euclidean remainder), and a tampered
negative-entry product is caught.
\item \texttt{test\_challenge\_derivation\_deterministic} --- prover and verifier derive
the \emph{identical} challenge from the same beacon seed, and different seeds give
different challenges (the reproducibility half of anti-replay,
Lemma~\ref{lem:anti-replay}).
\item \texttt{test\_wrong\_dims\_rejected} --- a shape mismatch fails \emph{closed} rather
than reading out of bounds (the fail-closed discipline of Section~\ref{sec:tiers}).
\end{itemize}

The production challenge derivation is keccak over the on-chain beacon (to match the
chain's wire spec); the module's in-test expansion is a \texttt{splitmix64} stream with
\emph{identical} soundness---what the soundness needs is that $r$ is fixed and
reproducible by both sides and unpredictable before commitment, which the beacon supplies
in production and the deterministic stream supplies for the isolated test.

\subsection{Built and tested: the two determinism prerequisites}

The exact-integer Freivalds check is bit-exact only if the surrounding decode is
reproducible; Section~\ref{sec:freivalds}'s determinism contract names the two places a
naive runtime injects nondeterminism, and both are now closed in the engine.

\paragraph{Lowest-token-id argmax tie-break (\texttt{hanzo-engine/src/sampler.rs}).}
Greedy decoding selects $\arg\max$ over the logits; on exactly-equal logits the winner
must be pinned or two honest nodes diverge. The engine's \texttt{argmax\_f32} now breaks
ties to the \emph{lowest} index (smallest token id) via a strict ``$>$'' scan that only
advances on a strictly greater value. This corrects Rust's \texttt{Iterator::max\_by},
which returns the \emph{last} maximal element (the highest id)---the opposite of what
reproducible consensus needs. This realizes clause~3 of the determinism contract
normatively in code.

\paragraph{Canonical MoE routing top-$k$ (\texttt{hanzo-engine/src/ops.rs}).}
\sloppy For MoE
models a different selected expert is a different sub-network, so prover and verifier
must select identical experts (Section~\ref{sec:moe-proof}). The engine adds a
process-wide proof-deterministic routing mode---\texttt{set\_moe\_\allowbreak proof\_\allowbreak
deterministic} / \texttt{moe\_\allowbreak proof\_\allowbreak deterministic}---which, when
enabled, forces a single canonical CPU path: \texttt{moe\_\allowbreak router\_\allowbreak topk}
computes F32 scores and selects the top-$k$ by a \emph{canonical, backend-independent}
tie-break (order each row by score descending then expert-index ascending, under an f32 total
order; \texttt{deterministic\_\allowbreak topk\_\allowbreak indices}),
rather than candle's unspecified \texttt{.topk()} tie-break or the separate CUDA
bf16/f16 router kernel. Off by default---normal inference keeps the fast path---it is
turned on for proof-bearing runs, and it is the routing analog of the lowest-id argmax
tie-break.

Each prerequisite is pinned by its own passing unit test
(\texttt{test\_argmax\_tie\_breaks\_to\_lowest\_id} in \texttt{sampler.rs},
\texttt{test\_deterministic\_moe\_routing\_tie\_break\_lowest\_expert} in \texttt{ops.rs}),
so the verifier core and the determinism contract it relies on are \textbf{8/8 passing}
together.

\subsection{Released and consumed on-chain}

The built-and-tested core is not confined to a feature branch: it is merged to the default
branch and tagged under public semantic versioning, and the chain-side verifier the
settlement layer consumes is published as a separate, canonical primitive.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
\item \textbf{Canonical verifier (\texttt{luxfi/crypto/poi}, \texttt{v1.19.23}).} The
verifier the chain consumes is a dependency-light Go package living beside the canonical
\texttt{Keccak256}: \texttt{Verify}/\texttt{VerifyMulti} over $\mathbb{F}_p$ ($p=2^{61}-1$)
on the exact integer accumulator, with keccak \texttt{DeriveChallenges}. The engine's
\texttt{poi.rs} is the prover-side mirror; the two must agree byte-for-byte.
\item \textbf{Prover-side engine (\texttt{hanzoai/engine}, \texttt{v1.2.5}).} The
\texttt{poi.rs} core and the two determinism prerequisites above, on the engine's released
line.
\item \textbf{On-chain enforcement (\texttt{luxdao/contracts}, \texttt{v2.1.0}).} The
settlement contracts gate every AI-work mint on a valid compute proof at the tier's
required proof type (\emph{no valid proof, no mint, no settle}): the \texttt{reportData}
binding library, the tier$\to$proof-type profile, the optimistic and confidential-compute
evidence backends, and an attestation-root registry. The two verified-cognition mint paths
(attested A-chain receipts and on-chain thinking-validator consensus) share one capped
coin under a joint halving, so adding a mint path never inflates past the cap.
\textbf{230/230} contract tests pass.
\item \textbf{Node binaries.} The prover-side core ships in the Rust inference
engine (\texttt{hanzo-engine} v1.2.5) and is inherited by the Hanzo node
(\texttt{hanzod}, which embeds the engine as a path dependency); the chain-side
verifier reaches the Go nodes (\texttt{luxd}, \texttt{zood}) through the
\texttt{luxfi/crypto/poi} dependency (v1.19.23), and EVM-level enforcement is
node-agnostic. What is \emph{not} yet wired into any node's forward path is the
emission of the activation-trace transcript---the next increment below.
\end{itemize}

What this establishes jointly with the core suite: the soundness primitive is correct in
isolation \emph{and} the economic gate that consumes it---reject any payout whose claimed
work carries no admissible proof---is implemented and tested at the contract layer. What it
does \emph{not} yet establish is end-to-end emission from a running model, which the next
subsection scopes.

\subsection{Designed, not yet built}

The following are specified in this paper and are the honest remaining work; none is
implemented at the time of writing.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
\item \textbf{The activation-trace commitment (MMR).} The keccak Merkle Mountain Range
over per-tensor digests (Section~\ref{sec:freivalds}) is designed; the engine today emits
logits without it. Its natural integration point is the synchronous inference precompile
already present in the execution client.
\item \textbf{Forward-pass integration.} The per-layer commit hooks that feed
$(A,B,C)$ operands into the verifier under challenge, and the streaming of the trace
during a real forward/backward pass, are designed; the \texttt{poi.rs} core is
deliberately decoupled from the forward pass so that the verifier could be tested before
the integration exists.
\item \textbf{The routing proof.} Routing \emph{determinism} is built (above); the routing
\emph{proof}---committing router logits, selected expert indices, and the sparse
activation pattern, and rechecking the top-$k$ at a challenged token
(Section~\ref{sec:moe-proof})---is designed.
\item \textbf{The sequential-chaining proof.} Binding consecutive recurrent states (and,
identically, consecutive diffusion latents) in the commitment
(Sections~\ref{sec:ssm-proof}, \ref{sec:diffusion}) is designed.
\item \textbf{RepOps (pinned reduction tree).} The deterministic-floating-point mode for
the fp16/bf16/fp8 tier (Section~\ref{sec:repops}) is designed; the quantized path is what
is built and is what the \Zoo{} zoo ships.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}[What the tested core does and does not establish]
The 6/6 \texttt{poi.rs} suite establishes that the \emph{mathematical heart}---``you
cannot claim the matmul without doing it''---is implemented correctly over the exact
integer accumulator, including the sparse-cheat case, the signed-entry case, and the
fail-closed shape contract. It does \emph{not} by itself establish end-to-end inference
verification: that additionally requires the trace commitment and the forward-pass
integration above. We separate the two deliberately, in keeping with the decomplecting
discipline of the rest of the paper: the verifier is a value (a pure function of
operands and a challenge) that can be proven correct independently of \emph{where} the
operands come from, and the integration is a distinct concern that supplies those
operands. The determinism prerequisites being built and tested is what makes the
``re-derive and compare'' premise (Section~\ref{sec:determinism}) actual rather than
aspirational for the greedy and MoE-routing cases.
\end{remark}
